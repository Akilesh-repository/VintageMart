The Lost Art of
French Polishing.

Discover the meticulous process of French polishing, a technique dating back to the 18th century, and why it remains the gold standard for finishing high-end antique furniture today.

Read Article Back to Journal
Part I: Materials

The Chemistry of Shellac

Long before the invention of synthetic polyurethanes and spray lacquers, the master cabinetmakers of the 18th and 19th centuries relied on a remarkable, naturally occurring substance to protect and beautify their creations. This substance is shellac. To truly understand the lost art of French polishing, one must first appreciate the unique chemistry and origin of the material at its core.

Shellac is not a synthetic resin, nor is it extracted from a plant like linseed oil or carnauba wax. Remarkably, it is an animal product. It is a resin secreted by the female lac bug (Kerria lacca), native to the forests of India and Thailand. These insects secrete the resin onto the branches of host trees to form tunnel-like tubes. This raw "sticklac" is harvested, crushed, washed, and refined into the brittle, golden-amber flakes that sit in glass jars on our restoration benches today.

When these flakes are dissolved in high-proof denatured alcohol, they create a liquid finish that possesses qualities no modern chemical can replicate. Unlike modern varnishes that sit entirely on top of the wood like a sheet of plastic, shellac penetrates the upper cellular structure of the timber. As the alcohol evaporates, it leaves behind a microscopically thin, incredibly hard layer of natural resin.

This unique physical property is why French polishing is still the gold standard for high-end antique restoration and acoustic instruments like violins and grand pianos. Because the finish bonds so intimately with the wood, it flexes and moves with the timber across decades of seasonal humidity changes. More importantly, it possesses an unmatched optical depth. A proper shellac finish does not merely reflect light off the surface; it allows light to travel through the resin, illuminating the chatoyancy and three-dimensional figure of woods like mahogany, rosewood, and burled walnut from within.

Part II: Technique

The Mastery of The Rubber

If shellac is the medium, then the "fad" (or "rubber") is the brush. French polishing is entirely distinct from painting or wiping on a finish. It is a grueling, intensely physical process of friction-application that demands a sensitivity to temperature, humidity, and the tactile feedback of the wood itself. It cannot be mechanized.

The rubber is constructed by folding a piece of lint-free cotton wadding into a tight, pear-shaped core. This core acts as the reservoir for the shellac. The core is then wrapped tightly in a square of fine, well-washed linen or cotton. The craftsman charges the core with shellac from the back, twists the linen tight to force the liquid through to the flat bottom face, and applies a single drop of linseed oil to the surface to act as a lubricant.

The application begins with "fadding in" or "bodying." The artisan moves the rubber across the wood in continuous, overlapping figures-of-eight or tight spirals. The rubber must never stop moving while in contact with the surface; if it pauses for even a fraction of a second, the alcohol in the pad will instantly dissolve the underlying layers, burning a permanent, ugly ring into the finish that ruins hours of labor.

A flawless mirror finish requires the application of literally hundreds of these microscopic layers over the course of several days or weeks. Between sessions, the piece must be left to "spirit off" and harden. Very fine pumice powder is periodically dusted onto the surface during the early stages; the friction of the rubber grinds the pumice and shellac into a paste that perfectly fills the open pores of the wood, creating a surface as smooth as glass.

The final stage, "spiriting off," requires immense skill. The rubber is charged almost entirely with pure alcohol rather than shellac. With the lightest possible touch, the artisan glides over the surface to pull out the residual lubricating oil and level any microscopic ridges left by previous passes. When executed correctly by a master polisher, the resulting surface is breathtaking—a warm, liquid mirror that serves as a testament to the enduring beauty of human craftsmanship.
